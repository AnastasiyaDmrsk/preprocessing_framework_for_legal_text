The process begins with an initial event. Following this, entities are required to notify the CSIRT (Computer Security Incident Response Team) or a competent authority about any significant incident. Subsequently, entities should inform the recipients of their services if it is deemed appropriate.

Next, entities are tasked with reporting any cross-border impact information. The competent authority then forwards this notification to the CSIRT, if applicable. Following this, single points of contact receive relevant information concerning cross-border incidents.

Entities are then instructed to communicate any measures or remedies to affected recipients in cases of cyber threats. Additionally, entities should inform recipients about the cyber threat itself, if appropriate.

At this point, a decision is made. If the incident is not considered significant, the process concludes. If the incident is significant, an early warning must be submitted within 24 hours. Following this, an incident notification needs to be submitted within 72 hours.

Another decision point is reached. If requested, an intermediate report is submitted. For trust service providers, there is a specific notification requirement within 24 hours. For other entities, the submission of an intermediate report upon request is applicable.

After the intermediate report, a final report is submitted within one month. A further decision is made regarding the incident's status. If the incident is ongoing, progress reports and a final report are provided. If the incident is resolved, the process moves to the submission of the final report.

Concurrently, the CSIRT or competent authority provides a response and guidance within 24 hours of the early warning. The CSIRT may offer additional technical support if requested. The CSIRT or competent authority also provides guidance on reporting to law enforcement if a criminal nature is suspected.

The CSIRT, competent authority, or single point of contact then informs other affected Member States and ENISA (European Union Agency for Cybersecurity). They also ensure the preservation of security and commercial interests.

A decision is made regarding public awareness. If public awareness is deemed necessary, the public is informed, or the entity is required to inform the public. If no public awareness is needed, this step is skipped.

The single point of contact forwards notifications to other Member States upon request. Subsequently, the single point of contact submits a summary report to ENISA every three months.

CSIRTs and competent authorities provide information to competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557. The Commission adopts implementing acts that specify notification details. Furthermore, the Commission adopts implementing acts specifying significant incident cases for certain providers by October 2024. The Commission also exchanges advice and cooperates with the Cooperation Group.

The process concludes with a final event.
