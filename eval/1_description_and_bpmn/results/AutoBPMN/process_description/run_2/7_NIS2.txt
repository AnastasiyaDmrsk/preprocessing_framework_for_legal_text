The process begins with an initial event. Following this, entities are required to notify the CSIRT (Computer Security Incident Response Team) or a competent authority about any significant incident. Subsequently, these entities must inform the recipients of their services if it is deemed appropriate.

Afterward, entities are to report any cross-border impact information. The competent authority then forwards this notification to the CSIRT, if applicable. Single points of contact receive relevant information concerning cross-border incidents.

Entities are then instructed to communicate any measures or remedies to affected recipients in cases of cyber threats. Additionally, entities should inform recipients about the cyber threat itself, if appropriate.

At this point, a decision is made. If the incident is deemed significant, an early warning must be submitted within 24 hours. Following this, an incident notification needs to be submitted within 72 hours.

Another decision point is reached. If requested, an intermediate report is to be submitted. Following this, there are two possible paths. One path involves submitting a final report within one month. The other path is for trust service providers, who must notify within 24 hours. Both paths eventually lead to the submission of a final report within one month.

After the final report is submitted, a further decision is made based on the incident's status. If the incident is ongoing, progress reports and a final report are to be provided. If the incident is resolved, the process moves directly to the submission of the final report.

Once the final report is submitted, the CSIRT or competent authority provides a response and guidance within 24 hours of the early warning. The CSIRT may offer additional technical support if requested. The CSIRT or competent authority also provides guidance on reporting to law enforcement if a criminal nature is suspected.

The CSIRT, competent authority, or single point of contact then informs other affected Member States and ENISA (European Union Agency for Cybersecurity). They also ensure that security and commercial interests are preserved.

A decision is then made regarding public awareness. If public awareness is deemed necessary, the public is informed about the incident, or the entity is required to do so. If public awareness is not needed, this step is skipped.

Following this, the single point of contact forwards notifications to other Member States upon request. Subsequently, the single point of contact submits a summary report to ENISA every three months.

CSIRTs or competent authorities provide information to competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557. The Commission then adopts implementing acts specifying notification details. The Commission also adopts implementing acts for specific entities by October 2024. Finally, the Commission exchanges advice with the Cooperation Group.

The process concludes with an end event. There is also a path where if the incident is not significant, the process bypasses several reporting steps and directly leads to the end event.
