The process begins with an initial event. Following this, entities are required to notify the CSIRT (Computer Security Incident Response Team) or competent authority about a significant incident. Subsequently, the entities must notify recipients of their services if it is deemed appropriate.

Afterward, entities are to report any cross-border impact information. The competent authority then forwards the notification to the CSIRT, if applicable. Following this, single points of contact receive relevant information concerning cross-border incidents.

At this juncture, a decision point is reached. If the incident is not significant, the process concludes. However, if the incident is significant, the process continues. Entities are then expected to communicate the measures or remedies they are taking to affected recipients. Following this, entities inform recipients about the cyber threat, if appropriate.

Another decision point is encountered, leading to different reporting paths based on the nature and timing of the incident. These paths include:
* Submitting an early warning within 24 hours, including potential causes and cross-border impact.
* Submitting an incident notification within 72 hours, detailing the assessment, severity, impact, and Indicators of Compromise (IOCs).
* Submitting an intermediate report on status updates, if requested.
* Submitting a final report within one month, providing a detailed description, root cause, mitigation strategies, and cross-border impact.
* Submitting a progress report and a final report after handling an ongoing incident.

Regardless of the specific reporting path taken, the process then proceeds to the CSIRT or competent authority, which provides a response and guidance.

Following this, another decision point determines the subsequent actions. The CSIRT or competent authority may inform other affected Member States and ENISA (European Union Agency for Cybersecurity) if appropriate. In this case, the single point of contact forwards notifications to other Member States if requested, and then submits a summary report to ENISA every three months. Additionally, the CSIRT or competent authority provides information to competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557. The Commission then adopts implementing acts regarding notification details.

Alternatively, the CSIRT or competent authority may provide guidance on reporting to law enforcement.

A further decision point considers whether public awareness is necessary. If public awareness is deemed necessary, the CSIRT or competent authority informs the public or requires the entity to do so. If public awareness is not needed, this step is bypassed.

Finally, after these potential actions, the process reaches its conclusion.
