The process contains the following actors: Essential and important entities (with 2 lanes: General essential and important entities, Trust service providers), CSIRT, Competent authority, Single point of contact, ENISA, Recipients of services, Competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557, The Commission, Cooperation Group, CSIRTs network.
The process starts with an essential or important entity becoming aware of an incident.
The essential or important entity shall assess whether the incident is significant. An incident shall be considered significant if: (a) it has caused or is capable of causing severe operational disruption of the services or financial loss for the entity concerned; or (b) it has affected or is capable of affecting other natural or legal persons by causing considerable material or non-material damage. If the incident is not significant, the process for the Essential and important entities ends.
If the incident is significant, the essential or important entity shall carry out the following 2 activities in parallel: (1) The entity shall submit an early warning to the CSIRT or, where applicable, the competent authority, without undue delay and in any event within 24 hours of becoming aware of the significant incident. The early warning shall, where applicable, indicate whether the significant incident is suspected of being caused by unlawful or malicious acts or could have a cross-border impact. The entity shall report, inter alia, any information enabling the CSIRT or, where applicable, the competent authority to determine any cross-border impact of the incident. The mere act of notification shall not subject the notifying entity to increased liability. (2) The entity shall, where appropriate, notify the recipients of its services, without undue delay, of significant incidents that are likely to adversely affect the provision of those services. Both activities shall be completed before the next step.
The entity shall assess whether it notified the competent authority rather than the CSIRT. If the entity notified the competent authority, the competent authority shall forward the notification to the CSIRT upon receipt. If the entity notified the CSIRT directly, no forwarding is required.
The entity shall assess whether the significant incident is cross-border or cross-sectoral. If the incident is cross-border or cross-sectoral, the entity shall ensure that the single point of contact is provided in due time with the relevant information notified in accordance with paragraph 4.
The entity shall assess whether a significant cyber threat exists that potentially affects the recipients of its services. If such a threat exists, the entity shall communicate, without undue delay, to the recipients of its services any measures or remedies that those recipients are able to take in response to that threat. The entity shall, where appropriate, also inform those recipients of the significant cyber threat itself. If no such threat exists, no communication to recipients is required.
The essential or important entity (General lane) shall submit an incident notification to the CSIRT or, where applicable, the competent authority, without undue delay and in any event within 72 hours of becoming aware of the significant incident. The incident notification shall update the information referred to in the early warning and shall indicate an initial assessment of the significant incident, including its severity and impact, as well as, where available, the indicators of compromise. By way of derogation, a trust service provider (Trust service providers lane) shall submit the incident notification to the CSIRT or, where applicable, the competent authority, without undue delay and in any event within 24 hours of becoming aware of the significant incident, except where the entity is not a trust service provider.
The entity shall submit an intermediate report on relevant status updates upon the request of the CSIRT or, where applicable, the competent authority.
The entity shall submit a final report to the CSIRT or, where applicable, the competent authority, not later than one month after the submission of the incident notification. The final report shall include: (i) a detailed description of the incident, including its severity and impact; (ii) the type of threat or root cause that is likely to have triggered the incident; (iii) applied and ongoing mitigation measures; (iv) where applicable, the cross-border impact of the incident.
The entity shall assess whether the incident is ongoing at the time of submission of the final report. If the incident is ongoing, the entity shall submit a progress report at that time instead of the final report. The entity shall submit a final report within one month of their handling of the incident. If the incident is not ongoing, the process for the Essential and important entities ends with the Final report submission end event.
The process for the CSIRT starts upon receiving the early warning or the notification from the essential or important entity, or upon receiving the forwarded notification from the competent authority.
The CSIRT or the competent authority shall provide, without undue delay and where possible within 24 hours of receiving the early warning, a response to the notifying entity. The response shall include initial feedback on the significant incident. The CSIRT or the competent authority shall, upon request of the entity, provide guidance or operational advice on the implementation of possible mitigation measures. Where the CSIRT is not the initial recipient of the notification, the guidance shall be provided by the competent authority in cooperation with the CSIRT.
The CSIRT shall provide additional technical support if the entity concerned so requests.
The CSIRT shall assess whether the significant incident is suspected to be of criminal nature. If the incident is suspected to be of criminal nature, the CSIRT or the competent authority shall provide guidance to the notifying entity on reporting the significant incident to law enforcement authorities. If the incident is not suspected to be of criminal nature, no such guidance is required.
The CSIRT shall assess whether the significant incident concerns two or more Member States. If the incident concerns two or more Member States, the CSIRT, the competent authority, or the single point of contact shall inform, without undue delay, the other affected Member States and ENISA of the significant incident. Such information shall include the type of information received in accordance with paragraph 4. The CSIRT, the competent authority, or the single point of contact shall, in accordance with Union or national law, preserve the entity's security and commercial interests as well as the confidentiality of the information provided. If the incident does not concern two or more Member States, no cross-border notification is required.
The CSIRT shall assess whether public awareness is necessary to prevent the significant incident or to deal with an ongoing significant incident, or whether disclosure is otherwise in the public interest. If public awareness is necessary or disclosure is in the public interest, the CSIRT or, where applicable, the competent authority may, after consulting the entity concerned, inform the public about the significant incident or require the entity to do so. If public awareness is not necessary and disclosure is not in the public interest, no public information is issued.
The CSIRT or, where applicable, the competent authority shall provide to the competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557 information about significant incidents, incidents, cyber threats, and near misses notified in accordance with paragraph 1 and with Article 30 by entities identified as critical entities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557.
The process for the CSIRT ends.
The process for the Competent authority starts upon receiving the notification from the essential or important entity, where applicable.
The competent authority shall forward the notification to the CSIRT upon receipt.
The competent authority shall, where the CSIRT is not the initial recipient of the notification, provide guidance or operational advice on the implementation of possible mitigation measures, in cooperation with the CSIRT.
The competent authority shall assess whether the significant incident is suspected to be of criminal nature. If the incident is suspected to be of criminal nature, the competent authority shall provide guidance to the notifying entity on reporting the significant incident to law enforcement authorities. If the incident is not suspected to be of criminal nature, no such guidance is required.
The competent authority shall, where appropriate and where the significant incident concerns two or more Member States, inform the other affected Member States and ENISA of the significant incident, without undue delay.
The competent authority may, where public awareness is necessary or disclosure is in the public interest, after consulting the entity concerned, inform the public about the significant incident or require the entity to do so.
The competent authority shall provide to the competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557 information about significant incidents, incidents, cyber threats, and near misses notified by entities identified as critical entities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557.
The process for the Competent authority ends.
The process for the Single point of contact starts upon receiving relevant information about a cross-border or cross-sectoral significant incident.
The single point of contact shall, at the request of the CSIRT or the competent authority, forward notifications received pursuant to paragraph 1 to the single points of contact of other affected Member States.
The single point of contact shall, where appropriate and where the significant incident concerns two or more Member States, inform the other affected Member States and ENISA of the significant incident, without undue delay.
At least every three months, the single point of contact shall submit to ENISA a summary report including anonymised and aggregated data on significant incidents, incidents, cyber threats, and near misses notified in accordance with paragraph 1 and with Article 30.
The process for the Single point of contact ends with the Summary report submission end event.
The process for ENISA starts upon receiving the summary report from the single point of contact or upon receiving information about a cross-border significant incident from the CSIRT, the competent authority, or the single point of contact.
ENISA may adopt technical guidance on the parameters of the information to be included in the summary report, in order to contribute to the provision of comparable information.
At least every six months, ENISA shall inform the Cooperation Group and the CSIRTs network about its findings on notifications received.
The process for ENISA ends with the Findings dissemination end event.
The process for Recipients of services starts upon receiving notification from the essential or important entity about a significant incident likely to adversely affect the provision of services, or upon receiving communication about measures or remedies in response to a significant cyber threat.
Upon receiving notification of a significant incident, the recipients of services are informed of the adverse impact on service provision. Upon receiving communication about a significant cyber threat, the recipients of services are informed of measures or remedies they are able to take in response to the threat and, where appropriate, of the significant cyber threat itself.
The process for the Recipients of services ends.
The process for the Competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557 starts upon receiving information from the CSIRT or, where applicable, the competent authority.
Upon receiving information, the competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557 are informed about significant incidents, incidents, cyber threats, and near misses notified by entities identified as critical entities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557.
The process for the Competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557 ends.
The process for the Commission starts independently.
The Commission may adopt implementing acts further specifying the type of information, the format, and the procedure of a notification submitted pursuant to paragraph 1 and to Article 30, and of a communication submitted pursuant to paragraph 2.
By 17 October 2024, the Commission shall, with regard to DNS service providers, TLD name registries, cloud computing service providers, data centre service providers, content delivery network providers, managed service providers, managed security service providers, as well as providers of online marketplaces, of online search engines, and of social networking services platforms, adopt implementing acts further specifying the cases in which an incident shall be considered to be significant. The Commission may adopt such implementing acts with regard to other essential and important entities.
The Commission shall exchange advice and cooperate with the Cooperation Group on the draft implementing acts in accordance with Article 14(4), point (e).
The implementing acts shall be adopted in accordance with the examination procedure referred to in Article 39(2).
The process for the Commission ends with the Implementing acts adoption end event.
The process for the Cooperation Group starts upon receiving findings from ENISA or upon being consulted by the Commission on draft implementing acts.
Upon receiving findings from ENISA, the Cooperation Group is informed about findings on notifications received. Upon being consulted by the Commission, the Cooperation Group participates in the exchange of advice and cooperation on draft implementing acts.
The process for the Cooperation Group ends.
The process for the CSIRTs network starts upon receiving findings from ENISA.
Upon receiving findings from ENISA, the CSIRTs network is informed about findings on notifications received every six months.
The process for the CSIRTs network ends.
