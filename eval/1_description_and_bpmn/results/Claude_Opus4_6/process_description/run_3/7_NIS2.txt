The process contains the following actors: Essential and important entities (with lane: Trust service providers), CSIRT, Competent authority, Single point of contact, Recipients of services, ENISA, Cooperation Group, CSIRTs network, Competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557, Law enforcement authorities, and the European Commission.

The process starts with an essential or important entity becoming aware of an incident.

The process for the Essential and important entities starts. The entity shall assess whether the incident is significant. An incident shall be considered significant if (a) it has caused or is capable of causing severe operational disruption of the services or financial loss for the entity concerned, or (b) it has affected or is capable of affecting other natural or legal persons by causing considerable material or non-material damage. If the incident is not significant, the process for the entity ends. If the incident is significant, the entity shall notify, without undue delay, its CSIRT or, where applicable, its competent authority. The mere act of notification shall not subject the notifying entity to increased liability. The entity shall report any information enabling the CSIRT or, where applicable, the competent authority to determine any cross-border impact of the incident. The entity shall carry out the following notification activities in sequence: First, the entity shall submit to the CSIRT or, where applicable, the competent authority an early warning without undue delay and in any event within 24 hours of becoming aware of the significant incident. The early warning shall, where applicable, indicate whether the significant incident is suspected of being caused by unlawful or malicious acts or could have a cross-border impact. Second, the entity shall submit an incident notification without undue delay and in any event within 72 hours of becoming aware of the significant incident. The incident notification shall, where applicable, update the information referred to in the early warning and indicate an initial assessment of the significant incident, including its severity and impact, as well as, where available, the indicators of compromise. By way of derogation, in the Trust service provider lane, a trust service provider shall submit the incident notification without undue delay and in any event within 24 hours of becoming aware of the significant incident. Third, upon the request of the CSIRT or, where applicable, the competent authority, the entity shall submit an intermediate report on relevant status updates. Fourth, the entity shall submit a final report not later than one month after the submission of the incident notification. The final report shall include (i) a detailed description of the incident, including its severity and impact, (ii) the type of threat or root cause that is likely to have triggered the incident, (iii) applied and ongoing mitigation measures, and (iv) where applicable, the cross-border impact of the incident. The entity shall assess whether the incident is ongoing at the time of submission of the final report. If the incident is ongoing, the entity shall provide a progress report at that time. The entity shall then provide a final report within one month of its handling of the incident. If the incident is not ongoing, the final report concludes the notification sequence. The entity shall, where appropriate, notify the recipients of its services of significant incidents that are likely to adversely affect the provision of those services. Where applicable, the entity shall communicate, without undue delay, to the recipients of its services that are potentially affected by a significant cyber threat any measures or remedies that those recipients are able to take in response to that threat. Where appropriate, the entity may also inform those recipients of the significant cyber threat itself. The process for the Essential and important entities ends.

The process for the Competent authority starts. Upon receiving a significant incident notification from an entity, where the entity notified the competent authority rather than the CSIRT, the competent authority shall forward the notification to the CSIRT upon receipt. Where the CSIRT is not the initial recipient of the notification, the competent authority shall provide guidance to the notifying entity in cooperation with the CSIRT. Where appropriate, and in particular where the significant incident concerns two or more Member States, the competent authority shall inform, without undue delay, the other affected Member States and ENISA of the significant incident. Such information shall include the type of information received in accordance with the notification requirements. In so doing, the competent authority shall, in accordance with Union or national law, preserve the entity's security and commercial interests as well as the confidentiality of the information provided. Where public awareness is necessary to prevent a significant incident or to deal with an ongoing significant incident, or where disclosure of the significant incident is otherwise in the public interest, the competent authority may, after consulting the entity concerned, inform the public about the significant incident or require the entity to do so. The competent authority shall provide to the competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557 information about significant incidents, incidents, cyber threats and near misses notified by entities identified as critical entities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557. The process for the Competent authority ends.

The process for the CSIRT starts. Upon receiving an early warning, the CSIRT shall provide, without undue delay and where possible within 24 hours of receiving the early warning, a response to the notifying entity. The response shall include initial feedback on the significant incident. Upon request of the entity, the CSIRT shall include guidance or operational advice on the implementation of possible mitigation measures. The CSIRT shall provide additional technical support if the entity concerned so requests. The CSIRT shall assess whether the significant incident is suspected to be of criminal nature. If the significant incident is suspected to be of criminal nature, the CSIRT shall provide guidance on reporting the significant incident to law enforcement authorities. If the significant incident is not suspected to be of criminal nature, no additional guidance on law enforcement reporting is required. Where appropriate, and in particular where the significant incident concerns two or more Member States, the CSIRT shall inform, without undue delay, the other affected Member States and ENISA of the significant incident. Such information shall include the type of information received in accordance with the notification requirements. In so doing, the CSIRT shall, in accordance with Union or national law, preserve the entity's security and commercial interests as well as the confidentiality of the information provided. Where public awareness is necessary to prevent a significant incident or to deal with an ongoing significant incident, or where disclosure of the significant incident is otherwise in the public interest, the CSIRT may, after consulting the entity concerned, inform the public about the significant incident or require the entity to do so. The CSIRT shall provide to the competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557 information about significant incidents, incidents, cyber threats and near misses notified by entities identified as critical entities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557. The process for the CSIRT ends.

The process for the Single point of contact starts. The single point of contact shall receive, in due time, relevant information regarding cross-border or cross-sectoral significant incidents notified in accordance with the notification requirements. At the request of the CSIRT or the competent authority, the single point of contact shall forward notifications to the single points of contact of other affected Member States. At least every three months, the single point of contact shall submit to ENISA a summary report including anonymised and aggregated data on significant incidents, incidents, cyber threats and near misses. The process for the Single point of contact ends.

The process for the Recipients of services starts. Upon receiving notification of a significant incident from an entity, the recipients of services shall take note of the information provided. Upon receiving communication regarding a significant cyber threat, the recipients of services shall take note of the measures or remedies that they are able to take in response to that threat. The process for the Recipients of services ends.

The process for the ENISA starts. ENISA shall receive the summary reports from single points of contact. ENISA may adopt technical guidance on the parameters of the information to be included in the summary report, in order to contribute to the provision of comparable information. At least every six months, ENISA shall inform the Cooperation Group and the CSIRTs network about its findings on notifications received. The process for the ENISA ends.

The process for the Cooperation Group starts. Upon receiving findings from ENISA, the Cooperation Group shall take note of the information provided. The Cooperation Group shall cooperate with the Commission on the draft implementing acts in accordance with Article 14(4), point (e). The process for the Cooperation Group ends.

The process for the CSIRTs network starts. Upon receiving findings from ENISA, the CSIRTs network shall take note of the information provided. The process for the CSIRTs network ends.

The process for the Competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557 starts. Upon receiving information from the CSIRT or the competent authority about significant incidents, incidents, cyber threats and near misses notified by entities identified as critical entities, the competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557 shall take note of the information provided. The process for the Competent authorities under Directive (EU) 2022/2557 ends.

The process for the Law enforcement authorities starts. Upon receiving a report of a significant incident suspected to be of criminal nature, the law enforcement authorities shall take note of the report. The process for the Law enforcement authorities ends.

The process for the European Commission starts. The Commission may adopt implementing acts further specifying the type of information, the format and the procedure of a notification submitted pursuant to the notification requirements and of a communication submitted pursuant to the cyber threat communication requirements. By 17 October 2024, the Commission shall, with regard to DNS service providers, TLD name registries, cloud computing service providers, data centre service providers, content delivery network providers, managed service providers, managed security service providers, as well as providers of online marketplaces, of online search engines and of social networking services platforms, adopt implementing acts further specifying the cases in which an incident shall be considered to be significant. The Commission may adopt such implementing acts with regard to other essential and important entities. The Commission shall exchange advice and cooperate with the Cooperation Group on the draft implementing acts. Those implementing acts shall be adopted in accordance with the examination procedure referred to in Article 39(2). The process for the European Commission ends.
